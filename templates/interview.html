    Introduction Round

    How to introduce yourself professionally

    Your introduction is the first 60-90 seconds of every interview. Make it count.

    01
    Who You Are
    State your name, current role or course of study. Keep it brief — one sentence maximum.
    02
    Relevant Experience
    Mention your most relevant experience or skills. Focus on what is relevant to the role you are applying for.
    03
    Why This Role
    Explain why you are interested in this specific role and company. Be genuine — not generic.
    04
    Your Key Strength
    End with one specific strength you bring that is directly relevant to the job. This makes you memorable.
    Example Introduction Structure

    Who I am: "My name is [Name] and I am a final year Computer Science student at [University]."

    Relevant experience: "I have built several web applications using Python and JavaScript, and recently completed an internship where I worked on backend APIs."

    Why this role: "I am applying because your company's focus on AI-driven products aligns perfectly with the projects I have been working on."

    Key strength: "My strongest quality is the ability to learn quickly — I taught myself three new frameworks in the last six months."

    Key Tips
    ✅
    Practise your introduction out loud until it sounds natural, not memorised.
    ✅
    Keep it focused on the role — do not list your entire life story.
    ✅
    Show enthusiasm — your energy in the first 60 seconds sets the tone for the whole interview.
    ❌
    Do not read from notes. Do not start with "Umm" or "So basically…"
    ❌
    Do not repeat everything on your CV. They have already read it.
    ⏱ Target time: 60 to 90 seconds. Under 45 seconds seems unprepared. Over 2 minutes loses the interviewer's attention.
    Behavioural Questions

    What are behavioural questions?

    Behavioural questions ask about things you have actually done in the past. Examples: "Tell me about a time when..." or "Describe a situation where..." — interviewers use these to predict how you will behave in future situations.

    💡 The key rule: Always answer with a real, specific example from your experience. Never answer hypothetically ("I would..."). Say what you actually did.

    Technical Questions

    What you need to know

    ⚠️
    Knowledge comes first
    To pass a technical interview you must have actual knowledge for the role you are applying for. No framework or technique will replace genuine understanding. Practise your craft, not just your answers.
    💡
    Answer short first, explain only if needed
    State your answer directly and concisely in 1-2 sentences. Only elaborate if the interviewer asks for more detail or if the context clearly requires it. Rambling suggests you are not confident.
    How to approach technical questions
    1. Listen carefully — make sure you understand the question before answering.
    2. Think briefly before speaking — 5-10 seconds of silence is professional, not weak.
    3. Answer directly — give your answer first, then support it.
    4. Be honest about gaps — "I haven't used that specific tool but I have experience with X which is similar" is far better than guessing.
    5. Show your thinking — interviewers often care more about how you think than the exact answer.
